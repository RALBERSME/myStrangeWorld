The man who voluntarily went to the concentration camp to stop the killing.

Witold Pilecki was born in 1901 in the Russian Empire.



His family was forcibly resettled there because his grandfather had participated in the January Uprising. During World War I, he joined a Polish self-defense unit.

Between 1919 and 1920, he participated in the Polish-Soviet War. Later, he joined the Polish Army. He was twice awarded the Cross of the Brave for his bravery.

In November 1939, he founded one of the first underground organizations in Nazi-occupied Poland. In 1940, he allowed himself to be infiltrated into Auschwitz I concentration camp as a prisoner to investigate what was going on there.

At that time, little was known about German activities in the camp. It was considered an internment camp. As prisoner number 4859, he continued to organize the defense underground. His plan was an internal uprising within the camp. Through him and his organization, information from the camp got out. He was also able to smuggle food and clothing into the camp for the inmates.

When his hopes for Allied intervention diminished, while the aggression of the Nazis in the concentration camp and throughout Poland increased, he decided to escape from the camp. During a night shift in the camp bakery, he and two comrades managed to escape with documents that he had stolen from the Germans. He wrote a detailed report on the conditions in the Auschwitz concentration camp.



In 1947, Pilecki was accused by the Soviet occupiers of Poland of spying for the Western Allies and executed ten days later. His grave has never been found. It is presumed that he was buried in a garbage dump near Warsaw.
